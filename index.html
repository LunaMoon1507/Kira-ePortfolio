03 — Cultural Reflection

Life in Seoul

What I learned culturally during my time abroad, and how the course shaped how I navigated the city.

Hierarchy & Confucian Values (유교)
Korean workplaces and social life are deeply shaped by Confucian principles of hierarchy and respect. I quickly learned the importance of addressing seniors by title, waiting for them to sit or begin eating first, and adapting my communication to be more formal and deferential. This was a significant shift from the relatively flat communication culture I was used to, and it pushed me to become more attuned to social cues.
빨리빨리 — The Fast-Paced Mindset
"Ppalli-ppalli" — meaning "hurry, hurry" — describes a cultural urgency that permeates everything from delivery services to workplace expectations. Seoul moves fast, and so do its people. I found this energizing as a developer; it mirrored the agile, ship-fast mentality I valued in my own work, though I also learned when to slow down and be deliberate.
Navigating Seoul Through the Course
GEB4941 gave me frameworks for reading cultural dimensions (like Hofstede's) that I could immediately apply on the subway, in convenience stores, at company dinners (회식), and during weekend market trips. Understanding the collective vs. individual mindset helped me approach everything from group decision-making at my internship to how I interacted with locals. The course turned observation into genuine cultural insight.
Food, Community, and Belonging
Sharing meals was one of the most meaningful cultural entry points. Whether it was samgyeopsal with coworkers or street tteokbokki in Hongdae, food was always communal, generous, and deeply intentional. These moments outside of the office were often where the most authentic cross-cultural exchange happened.

// Korean Workplace Culture
Working within a Korean professional environment taught me the value of consensus, patience, and collective harmony. Decision-making was rarely unilateral — ideas were discussed thoroughly across the team before action was taken. I also experienced 회식 (hoesik), the after-work team dinner culture, which deepened my understanding of how professional relationships are built in Korea: not just at the desk, but across the table.

// Lessons That Will Stay With Me
This experience fundamentally expanded my understanding of what it means to work globally. I returned not just with new technical skills, but with greater empathy, cultural humility, and the ability to adapt my communication and working style to different cultural contexts — skills that no classroom alone could have taught me.
